NatWest Interview Experience 
NatWest Group is a prominent UK-based banking and financial services company, serving over 19 
million customers across retail, commercial, and private banking markets. As the group's Global 
Capability Centre, NatWest Group India plays a vital role in driving innovation and providing world-
class services and solutions to support the bank's global customer base. 
Role Offered: Software Engineer 
Sequence of Selection: 
1. Online Test 
2. Technical + HR Interview (Offline) 
 
Online Test 
The online assessment consisted of four sections: 
• 
Coding: This section contained two easy-level questions based on arrays and strings. 
o 
Problem 1: A problem related to finding the sum of elements at alternate indexes in 
an array. 
o 
Problem 2: Given a vehicle number (can be a positive or negative integer), 
determine its "track number." The track number is the smallest numerical 
permutation of the vehicle number's digits, which should not start with a leading 
zero. 
• 
SQL: This section contained 15 easy multiple-choice questions. No query writing was 
required. 
• 
Aptitude: This section focused on business aptitude and visual reasoning, including 
questions on scheduling tasks, counting figures, interpreting pie charts, and identifying 
missing figures in a pattern. 
• 
English Proficiency: This section covered general English skills, including grammar, 
vocabulary, and reading comprehension. 
 
Interview Round (Offline - 1 Hour) 
The interview was conducted in-person with a panel of two interviewers and lasted for 
approximately one hour. The discussion flowed as follows: 
• 
Resume Walkthrough & Project Discussion: The interviewers started by asking me to give a 
brief about my resume. They then asked me to explain one of my projects in detail. 
o 
Project: I explained my E-Commerce Recommendation System project. 
o 
Follow-up Questions: They asked about the choice of tech stack and the biggest 
challenge I faced. They also noted the absence of a Machine Learning project on my 
resume and asked if I was not interested in ML. I explained that I had completed an 
ML project but chose to feature other real-world projects on my resume, and then I 
briefly explained the ML project as well. 
• 
Positional Experience (CSEA): The interviewers asked about my role as the Logistics Office 
Bearer for the Computer Science Engineering Association (CSEA). They asked me to describe 
the association and my specific contributions to it. 
• 
Data Structures Problem (Strings): 
o 
Problem: "Print all characters that appear only once in a given string." 
o 
My Approach: I initially suggested a solution using a HashMap in Java. However, the 
interviewers added a constraint not to use any inbuilt collection classes. I then 
pivoted to a solution using two loops and a frequency counter array to keep track of 
repeated characters. They asked if this was the most optimized solution under the 
given constraint, to which I replied that it was the best I could think of at the 
moment. 
• 
SQL Query: Before the question, they asked me to rate my skills in SQL. 
o 
Problem: Given a single table with columns (emp_id, date, status), write a query to 
display the details of all employees who had a "present" status for at least three 
consecutive days. 
o 
My Approach: I explained and wrote a query using a self-join on the same table 
twice to solve the problem. 
• 
Behavioral & Situational Questions: The interviewers then asked a few HR-style questions: 
o 
"Describe yourself." 
o 
"Explain a situation which made your friend move in a completely different 
direction." 
o 
"Explain a situation where you had to take responsibility for a team." 
• 
OOP Concepts: The final technical discussion was on Object-Oriented Programming, where I 
was asked to explain the four pillars of OOP (Encapsulation, Abstraction, Inheritance, and 
Polymorphism) using real-world examples. 
At the end of the interview, I asked the interviewers about the tech stack used at the company and 
the potential work location. 
 
Results 
The results were announced on the same day. 9 students were selected for a direct full-time offer. 
 
Takeaways 
• 
Strong knowledge in DSA, OS, CN, DBMS, and OOPS is required. 
• 
Prepare your self-introduction well. Try to include your interests, the tech stacks you know, 
the projects you have done, and any achievements you have. 
• 
For DSA questions, don’t jump directly to the optimal solution. First, explain the brute-force 
solution along with its time and space complexity, then move to the optimal solution, 
highlighting why it is better. Even if you don’t know the exact solution, share your thought 
process. 
• 
Be well-prepared on your projects—know them in detail, including challenges faced and 
how you solved them. 
• 
Always prepare a few questions to ask the interviewer at the end of the round. 
• 
Be confident and be prepared.
