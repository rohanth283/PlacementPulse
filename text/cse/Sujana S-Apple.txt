Coding Question 1: 
Restaurant Queue Management: Calculate the total profit of a restaurant with limited seats, 
where guests arrive and leave according to a log, pay only once, wait in a FIFO queue if seats 
are full, and leave if they lose patience. 
Eg: 
n = 3 
price = [6, 2, 9, 5, 8]    logs = [0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 1, 0, 3, 2, 4] 
Explanation: 
• 
n = 3: 3 seats available. 
• 
price[i]: Guest 0 → ₹6, Guest 1logs sequence: 
• 
0 arrives → seat available → seated 
• 
1 arrives → seat available → seated 
• 
2 arrives → seat available → seated 
• 
3 arrives → all seats full → waits 
• 
4 arrives → all seats full → waits 
• 
1 leaves → seat free → 3 seated 
• 
0 leaves → seat free → 4 seated 
• 
3 leaves → seat free 
• 
2 leaves → seat free 
• 
4 leaves → seat free  
• 
Seated Guests: 0, 1, 2, 3, 4 → all paid once. 
 
• 
Profit: 
6 + 2 + 9 + 5 + 8 = 30 
 
Coding Question 2: 
The task is to write a program that determines and returns the current state of a tennis game 
based on the points scored by two players. 
 Tennis Scoring Rules Recap: 
• 
A tennis game is scored in points: 
o 1st point → 15 
o 2nd point → 30 
o 3rd point → 40 
• 
After both players reach at least 40 points, three special conditions apply: 
1. 
Deuce: 
▪ 
When both players have scored the same number of points (and at least 
3 points each). 
▪ 
Example:   5–5. 
2. 
Advantage: 
▪ 
When both have at least 3 points, but one player has 1 point more than 
the other. 
▪ 
Example: 6–5 (Advantage Player 1) or 5–6 (Advantage Player 2). 
 
3. 
Win: 
▪ 
A player wins if they have at least 4 points and at least 2 points more 
than the opponent. 
▪ 
Example: 8–4 → Player 1 wins. 
Example: 
nameP1 = "Alice" 
nameP2 = "Bob" 
winner = {"Alice", "Bob", "Alice", "Alice"} 
Alice scores: 3 points (15 → 30 → 40)  Bob scores: 1 point (15) 
 Output: "40 - 15" 
{If more points are played and it becomes 4–4: 
 Output: "Deuce" 
If it becomes 5–4: 
 Output: "Advantage Alice" 
If it becomes 6–4: 
 Output: "Alice wins"} 
➔Even though the tennis game rules look lengthy, the implementation logic is actually 
very simple. The whole problem boils down to a few clear if–else conditions based on the 
scores of the two players. 
 
TECHNICAL INTERVIEW ROUND  1 (45 mins): 
The technical interview was conducted virtually. Initially, the interviewer asked me to 
introduce myself, followed by a few questions about my projects — particularly focusing on 
the unique contributions I had made to them. After that, the session moved to a pair coding 
round. The problem given was the "Restrictive Candy Crush" problem GFG. The interviewer 
clearly explained the problem statement and asked me to first describe my approach with a 
dry run of the test cases. 
I explained the stack-based approach, where each number and its count are stored and 
updated as we process the input. She then asked me to write the pseudocode and explain it 
step-by-step, followed by an analysis of the time and space complexity. She also asked me 
to suggest the possible test cases that could be designed for this problem to ensure 
correctness and handle edge scenarios. This part of the interview focused more on how 
interactively I was solving the problem and thinking through different situations. The 
interviewer was satisfied with my approach and explanation. 
Next, the discussion shifted to concepts related to Locks and Semaphores. I was asked to 
explain read-write locks, two-phase locking (with an example), and provide an overview of 
locks, semaphores, and concurrency. I explained each concept with suitable examples, and 
the interviewer was satisfied.

KEY TAKEAWAYS: 
1.  Give a strong and impressive self-introduction – it’s the most important part of the 
interview as it reflects who you are and sets the tone for the rest of the discussion. 
2. Know your project work thoroughly – interviewers are highly experienced, so you 
must be confident and well-prepared to discuss your contributions, challenges faced, 
and solutions implemented. 
3. Practice coding consistently on platforms like LeetCode and GFG, and focus deeply 
on Operating Systems, DBMS, OOPs, Computer Networks, and System Design – not 
just theoretically but also with practical applications, as interviewers often expect 
real-world examples. 
 Pitch yourself effectively – most of the questions will be based on what you say, so 
be thoughtful and strategic while presenting your skills and experiences. 
4. Research the company thoroughly before the interview – understanding its culture, 
values, products, and work environment helps you align your answers better.
