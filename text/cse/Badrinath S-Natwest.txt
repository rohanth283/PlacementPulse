Interview Experience – NatWest 
Hi everyone, I am Badrinath S (2022503017) and I am glad to share my NatWest 
interview experience. 
 
Round 1 – Online Assessment (On Campus) 
The initial screening test was conducted on SHL. It tested: 
• 
Basic English grammar and comprehension 
• 
Logical reasoning (including visual reasoning) 
• 
SQL (MCQs) 
• 
2 coding questions 
The first coding question was very easy: 
Print even numbers first and odd numbers next. 
Here, there was a small trick. If you use i % 2 == 1 for odd numbers, it will fail for 
negative integers. 
The second question was a medium-level greedy problem. I always use Java in coding 
interviews since many companies prefer it (as most backends are in Spring Boot). For 
this question, I created another class called Pair with three variables so that managing 
comparisons after using Collections.sort became easier. 
My tip for this round: 
• 
Do not waste time reading the entire long paragraph. Many companies add 
lengthy text to consume your time. Instead, first read the input format and 
required output. That will give you a clear idea of what the problem is about. 
• 
Once you understand the requirement, try to map the problem to a suitable data 
structure. 
• 
Be strong in basics, such as how to take input using the Scanner class. 
• 
If your code does not work immediately, don’t panic. Use console print 
statements to debug and trace the error. 
• 
Above all, stay honest throughout your placement season. That’s the best way to 
prepare better and shine. 
• 
Prepare aptitude through mock test in SHL portal. 
I got shortlisted along with 23 others for the next round. 
 
Round 2 – Technical + HR (Combined, 45 minutes) 
Both interviewers were kind and introduced themselves at the beginning. They started 
by asking about the Pre-Placement Talk that happened just before the interviews. They 
also asked me if I knew about NatWest earlier. I replied that, as a cricket fanatic, I knew 
that NatWest had once sponsored the England Cricket Team. 
Next, they moved on to my resume. They asked about my projects and were impressed 
that they solved real-world problems. They said they liked my approach and mentioned 
that it aligned well with the company’s vision. At that point, I was confident that I had a 
good chance of receiving the offer. 
Then came the DSA round. They gave me a simple array question involving a HashSet. I 
explained my approach, and the interviewer suggested storing the index instead of the 
element. I explained that even then, the time complexity would remain O(N). The 
interviewer agreed and was satisfied. 
After that, they asked a SQL query to find the third highest mark. Initially, I wrote a 
nested query. The interviewer pointed out that its complexity could be reduced. I then 
suggested using ORDER BY DESC and picking the third record (I forgot the word 
“OFFSET,” but the interviewer mentioned it himself and said my approach was correct). 
They also asked a few basic ML questions—such as What is supervised learning? and 
What is an activation function?—based on my resume, which mentioned ML. 
Behavioral Round 
Next, they moved toward behavioral questions. 
They asked me about my view on learning new technologies. I replied that I am always 
open to learning anything necessary to solve a client’s problem. However, I also added 
that I am not the type of person who upskills without a clear need, as I prefer to focus 
my efforts where they bring value. 
They then asked me about my integrity. One question was: “What would you do if an 
ethically wrong person asked you to do something in the bank that is profitable for both 
the company and the bank?” 
I answered that I value honesty above all, as it is crucial for long-term success, even if 
shortcuts may give temporary relief. I also mentioned that if I had to reject an unethical 
request, I would do so firmly but with kindness, ensuring I handle the situation with 
respect. 
Another question was about AI. They asked me how I would use it. I responded that I 
would not allow AI to hamper my own thinking capabilities. Instead, I would use it to 
perform tasks where I already know the logical approach, thereby improving speed and 
execution. 
The interviewers were impressed with my responses. At the end, they asked if I had any 
questions. I politely said I didn’t have any. They told me they enjoyed the conversation, 
wished me all the best, and I left the room with a sense of gratitude. 
 
My Tips 
1. Always include ML in your resume if you have worked on it. Interviewers value 
that highly. 
2. Do not panic; stay calm and honest. 
3. Never over-exaggerate your projects. Interviewers are more impressed when you 
explain your projects truthfully and confidently. Explain your projects in the 
perspective of how they would solve a real life problem. 
4. Resume is the starting point: The flow of the interview was driven by my 
resume. Be ready to explain every project you include and link it to solving real-
world problems. 
5. Explain your thought process: In coding questions, the interviewer often looks 
at how you approach the problem rather than just the final solution. 
6. SQL efficiency matters: Knowing both a straightforward and an optimized 
approach shows maturity. 
7. Behavioral questions test alignment with values: Integrity, honesty, and 
adaptability were tested more than technical brilliance. Companies want people 
they can trust in sensitive domains like banking. 
8. AI and emerging tech: Instead of exaggerating, give a balanced view. Show 
openness to use new technologies while maintaining human reasoning. 
9. Confidence > perfection: Even when I forgot the keyword OFFSET, I handled it 
calmly. That mattered more than knowing every detail. 
 
 
Finally I got the offer along with 8  other canditates.Felt very relieved and was at peace 
after so much grind.
