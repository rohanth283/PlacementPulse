About The Company 
Citibank is the consumer banking division of Citigroup, one of the world's largest 
financial services organizations. Citibank offers a wide range of financial products and 
services to individuals, businesses, and institutions, including retail banking, wealth 
management, global transaction services, etc. Citibank has a significant international 
presence with operations in numerous countries and serves millions of customers 
globally. 
Citi CSC stands for Citi Solutions Centers. It is a global network of technology centers 
that provide support to Citi’s core businesses and clients around the world. The centers 
are in strategic locations across the globe, including Pune, India, where one of Citi’s 
largest technology centers is located. They deal with Markets & Securities, Risk & 
Capital Management, Cash Management, Trade & Treasury Services, Private Banking, 
Mobile, Compliance & Surveillance Technology, Finance and Risk Mitigation. 
Citi CSC came to our college to recruit for 2 of their domains: 
• 
International Consumer Group (ICG): This domain focused more on 
development. Knowledge on microservices, Java, Apache server, Kafka and app 
development were tested in the online round for students who chose this 
domain, and the coding round for this test included the coding of a form (don’t 
really remember the details). 
• 
Enterprise Operations & Technology (EO&T): This was the domain I picked as 
it aligned a lot more with the fields I am passionate about, i.e. cybersecurity and 
testing. The job description included tasks like risk analysis and mitigation, data 
protection, web and mobile app testing, API development and integration, 
analytics, and a lot more. I have explained the test and the interview process 
for this domain in detail. 
 
Online Round: 
Platform: SHL Amcat 
• 
Verbal Section: Consisted of questions on reading comprehension, grammar, 
vocabulary, and elementary grammar skills [14 questions – 20 mins] 
• 
Advanced Aptitude: Questions were based on number sequences, text encoding, 
blood relations, logic puzzles, etc. [12 questions – 12 minutes] 
• 
CS Fundamentals: Algorithms, basic DBMS, code error correction, finding the 
output, etc.   [12 questions – 12 minutes] 
• 
Coding: 2 questions (total 45 mins) – no. of negative numbers in an array, sum of 
prime numbers within a given range [since the questions were extremely easy, 
they could be completed in less than 5 minutes XD] 
 
Round 1: Tech Round [Two interviewers] 
• 
During the interview, we kicked things off with the standard "Tell us about 
yourself." However, the conversation took an interesting turn when they 
discovered I’m not originally from Chennai. One of the interviewers was curious 
about how I coped with the language barrier, considering that Tamil is the 
predominant language in Chennai. I explained that after a decade of living in 
Chennai, I had become decently fluent in Tamil. I also mentioned that I 
continued to learn and improve my language skills as it wasn’t my mother tongue. 
• 
Then, they delved into my resume, where I had listed problem-solving as one of 
my skills. They asked for a real-life example of how I had applied this skill, so I 
shared a relevant experience. 
• 
The interview also got technical with questions about file handling and database 
management systems (DBMS). For most of these questions, I had to walk them 
through the logic step by step. In one instance, they asked me to write an SQL 
query on paper. 
• 
Next, we ventured into discussions about data structures. They were curious 
about which data structures I was familiar with, and this led to conversations 
about linked lists and their related problems 
• 
I was asked to explain the different kinds of trees, and I had accidently 
mentioned AVL trees, and that sparked a more in-depth discussion. The 
interviewer delved into how AVL trees function, their significance, and their 
practical applications. 
• 
They also chose one of my projects and asked me to provide a detailed 
explanation. They were keen to know my thoughts on potential improvements for 
the project. 
• 
They inquired about my decision to pick the EO&T domain, wanting to 
understand my motivations and reasons for choosing this specific field. 
• 
They threw in a more casual question about my familiarity with Object-Oriented 
Programming (OOP) concepts. I mentioned that I had a basic understanding of 
OOP. 
• 
As the interview concluded, they gave me the opportunity to ask any questions I 
had in mind 
 
Round 2: Managerial Round [Two Interviewers] 
• 
The interview began with the customary request for a self-introduction. 
Subsequently, they inquired about my place of origin once again, seeking to 
understand more about my background. 
• 
The conversation then shifted towards a discussion about the backend 
mechanism of payment apps like GPay. While I didn't have a complete grasp of 
the topic, I made an effort to provide a reasonable answer. In response, the 
interviewer acknowledged my attempt and said I was close, and then patiently

• 
They were also curious about my pursuits outside of academics. Specifically,

my responsibilities to a teammate.

I had mentioned my certification as a piano player in my resume, which piqued 
their interest. They asked me about my music interests, including my preferred

The discussion turned to my technical interests, which I had highlighted in my 
resume. They noticed my inclination towards core subjects and asked why I

Given that I had expressed an interest in research in the "About Me" section of

how it aligned with my career growth.

Round 3: HR Round [One Interviewer] 
• 
The interview commenced with the interviewer introducing himself, setting a 
conversational tone. After that, he invited me to introduce myself and share a 
bit about my background. 
• 
We then discussed my experiences with Citi so far, as he inquired about how my 
journey with the company had been up to that point. 
• 
He asked me whether I had any relatives employed in the government or in Citi 
• 
One of the projects I had prominently featured on my resume involved a website 
designed to facilitate Food Donation Management. He was keen to know what 
had motivated me to choose this particular project and delve into its working. 
• 
Noticing that I had listed proficiency in around five languages on my resume, the 
interviewer inquired about the reasons behind my pursuit of multiple languages, 
wanting to understand the motivations behind each one. 
• 
As the conversation continued, he asked if there was any recent achievement in 
my life that I felt particularly proud of. 
• 
I was then asked about what my greatest weakness was, to which I elaborated 
on and mentioned ways in which I successfully battled it and how I’m still 
continuing to work on it. 
• 
Finally, the interview came to a close, and he provided me with the opportunity 
to ask any questions I might have about the company or the role. 
 
All the interviews took place on the same day, in-person at CUIC. 
There were about 20 students shortlisted from 100 who picked the EO&T domain, and

My Tips: 
• 
DO NOT PANIC. At least with respect to Citi, the interview process is really 
chilled out and there is absolutely no need to stress 
• 
Don’t spend too much time on a single aptitude question. And on certain 
platforms like Amcat, they do no let you switch between questions or go back to 
a previous question, so tackle the questions on the spot and wisely. 
• 
The hiring committee really values students who have clear-cut interests and 
goals; those who know exactly what and why they want to do it. 
• 
At the same time, they are looking for those who have a strong passion to learn 
and explore everything; no company wants rigid and the always-in-your-comfort-
zone kind of employees 
• 
Try at least attempting to answer all the questions thrown at you 
• 
If you are asked something you don’t know, try to give a basic logical answer and 
make sure to mention that it is something you are open to learning/improving and 
you haven’t had the opportunity to explore it just yet 
• 
I received easy coding questions, but that may not be the case with other 
domains and companies. I had close to no coding practice for the internship 
drive and that is something I regret. Make sure you start practicing well in 
advance 
• 
DO NOT lie on your resume. Interviewers will easily be able to figure out 
whether you have bluffed or not 
• 
Know what the company does, how your skillset will benefit them and how they 
can help your career advance 
• 
Know your projects inside-out, every little detail can be questioned. Know the 
shortcomings of your project beforehand so that you’re not caught by surprise 
• 
Do not put a rating or level for your technical skills. I had done that and it 
landed me into slight trouble.
