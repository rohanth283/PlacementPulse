including: 
o Aptitude: Logical reasoning, numerical problem-solving, and 
puzzles.

Interview: 
The interview began on a cordial note with the classic “Tell me about yourself” 
opener, giving me a chance to set the stage. Without much delay, the 
interviewer presented me with a simple DSA challenge: find the maximum 
element in an array. While this might sound like a warm-up problem, it was 
relevant to the analytical nature of the Quality Engineer role — after all, even 
testers need to think like problem-solvers. 
Next, we started discussing about my projects. One idea in particular caught 
the recruiter’s attention, and the conversation went from “Tell me more” to a 
full-on deep dive into the origin, functionality, and execution of the project. 
This set the tone for the next phase: testing knowledge. I explained the 
different types of testing I had implemented in my “Hospital Appointment 
System” Project, covering Unit Testing, Integration Testing, and Regression 
Testing. 
Then came the main stuff — questions about Artificial Intelligence and 
whether it could design all test cases. I stood my ground, explaining that while 
AI is powerful, it lacks the hands-on experience of a seasoned quality engineer 
and might hallucinate (yes, I used the word) or overlook tricky edge cases. 
When asked why AI couldn’t test a project it had developed, I explained that AI 
is trained on existing data, while a human tester uses real-world insight to 
design precise, context-aware test cases. The interviewer countered that AI 
could do it, but I held my stance — politely but firmly. 
We then shifted to Selenium, where I described how I used it to test a login 
page, walking the interviewer through the process from initiating a browser 
connection to gracefully shutting it down after testing. 
Before wrapping up, I asked about the secret behind Appian’s sustained 
success, and the interviewer’s answer was as flawless as their product pitch. 
Round Two (HR): This was a mix of introductions, “Why Appian?” and “Why 
this role?” — the standard HR essentials. 
Round Three (Final HR): A quick check on whether I was comfortable with the 
role and a subtle nudge to see if I preferred a developer position instead. I 
reaffirmed my interest in Quality Engineering and assured them I would align 
with the company’s principles. 
Tips: 
For a Quality Engineer Intern role, a solid understanding of the Software 
Testing Life Cycle (STLC), Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC), and core 
testing concepts is essential. Focus on fundamental DSA topics such as arrays, 
strings, hash maps, and sets, as these are typically sufficient for the coding 
portion. Familiarity with testing tools like Selenium is a valuable advantage. 
Having a basic understanding of CI/CD pipelines can also strengthen your 
profile. 
Be thoroughly prepared to discuss your projects in detail, as most technical 
questions will stem from your resume. Only list skills and topics in your resume 
that you are confident in, and ensure you can explain them clearly. Be clear in 
whatever you speak and BE CONFIDENT – your body language and attitude tells 
them a lot about you before you speak. Dress professionally, and ensure proper 
grooming to create a strong first impression.
