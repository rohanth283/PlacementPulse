WELLS FARGO (PLACEMENTS)  
Role: Program Associate 
Batch: 2020 – 2024 
Mode: Offline 
Number of Rounds: 4 
About the Company: 
Wells Fargo is a prominent multinational financial services company based 
in San Francisco, USA. They offer a wide range of services including banking, 
investments, credit cards, mortgages, as well as consumer and commercial 
finance. In India, they have offices in Chennai, Hyderabad, and Bangalore. 
Round 1: Online Assessment 
The online test was conducted on the SHL AMCAT platform and 
consisted of three sections: 
1. Verbal Aptitude 
2. Business Aptitude 
3. Coding Section 
This format is typical for Wells Fargo's online assessments. The verbal 
aptitude section tested vocabulary and comprehension. The business 
aptitude section focused on data interpretation from pie charts, bar charts, 
graphs, and stocks & exchanges. While these sections weren't particularly 
difficult, the time constraints made quick reading and analysis essential. 
The coding section featured three problems. The first two were quite 
easy, and most candidates could pass all test cases. The third problem was 
of medium difficulty, but the challenge was compounded by the use of an 
older C++ version, which didn’t support vectors. It was a graph-based 
problem with only two test cases. My code passed one of them, which 
turned out to be sufficient. 
Out of all candidates, 59 were selected for the next round, with 22 being 
added just an hour before the interviews through an additional shortlist. 
Tip:  
The aptitude section seemed to carry more weight than the coding 
part. I recommend starting to practice aptitude questions on platforms like 
IndiaBix and PrepInsta about a month before placements begin. For coding, 
LeetCode and GeeksforGeeks should be sufficient, but it's best to start 
learning DSA at least three months in advance, as online tests are becoming 
increasingly challenging. 
 
Round 2: Technical Interview 1 
In this round, the interviewer was friendly and helped me feel at ease, 
knowing it was my first interview. He started with a real-life problem that 
required sorting as a solution. I explained Merge Sort in detail, as it’s a good 
algorithm for in-depth discussion. He asked a few follow-up questions as 
well. 
We then discussed the differences between C++ and Java, and the 
interviewer dug deeper into this topic. Next, he moved on to system design 
and posed the problem statement: "Consider you're storing data about all 
Indians in a database. How would you design such a database? What factors 
would you consider?" I suggested a few solutions to improve access time, 
reduce latency, and enhance scalability. 
I felt I didn’t do well on the system design part, but the interviewer gave 
a positive response. 
 
Round 3: Technical Interview 2 
This round began with a basic question: "What is your favorite data 
structure?" I chose graphs and explained traversal algorithms. The 
interviewer asked several real-life questions, and I answered them using 
concepts like cycle detection, shortest path, and weighted graphs. 
The conversation then shifted to my projects. I walked him through the 
entire process, and he asked about alternatives to the stack I used and the 
innovations I implemented. We also covered some HR questions, like where 
I see myself in 3-5 years, handling conflicts within a team, and task 
assignments. 
Overall, this round went smoothly. The interviewer seemed pleased, 
and his feedback was positive. 
Tip:  
To succeed in technical interviews, it’s important to clearly explain 
your ideas and solutions. Don’t let anxiety or nerves get the best of you; 
mock interviews can help build confidence. Be thoroughly familiar with 
everything on your resume, as most questions will be asked from it. 
 
Round 4: Behavioral + HR Round 
This round was more like a conversation than a formal interview. I was 
expecting the usual HR questions, but the interviewer seemed genuinely 
interested in getting to know me. She asked about my passions and how my 
college experience has shaped my character. Since she was also involved 
in the pre-placement talk, I asked her a few questions based on that, and 
she seemed impressed with my attentiveness. 
The HR round lasted about 20 minutes and ended on a positive note. 
In total, 20 students were selected.
