CITI SUMMER INTERNSHIP INTERVIEW EXPERIENCE 
Company & Role Overview 
• 
Company: Citi (Citigroup) is a leading global financial services company 
headquartered in New York. Founded in 1812, it offers a wide range of services 
including banking, investment banking, and wealth management to individuals, 
corporations, and governments in over 100 countries. 
• 
Role Offered: International Clients Group (ICG) Technology 
• 
Other Role: Personal Banking & Wealth Management Technology (PBWMT) 
• 
Stipend: ₹75,000 per month 
• 
Internship Duration: 8 weeks 
Selection Process 
1. Online Assessment 
2. Technical Interview 
3. HR Interview 
Round 1: Online Assessment (September 27, 2024) 
The online test was 90 minutes long and consisted of four sections. 
Important Note: It was not possible to switch between sections; they had to be completed in 
the order they were presented. 
Test Structure: 
• 
Coding: 2 questions (45 minutes) 
• 
SQL MCQs: 12 questions (15 minutes) 
• 
React MCQs: 12 questions (15 minutes) 
• 
Angular MCQs: 12 questions (15 minutes) 
Section Details: 
• 
Coding Questions - The questions were considered easy and were based on arrays. 
• 
SQL Questions 
o The difficulty level was easy to medium. 
• 
React & Angular JS Questions 
o These multiple-choice sections included questions about finding missing terms 
and identifying correct code statements. 
Following the online assessment, 28 candidates were shortlisted for the next round of 
interviews for the ICG Technology role. 
Round 2: Technical Interview (Duration: 1 hour) 
The interview began with my introduction. I structured it to highlight my favourite subjects 
and fields of interest, specifically mentioning my proficiency in Database Management 
Systems (DBMS). This led the interviewer to start with questions about my DBMS project. 
Project 1: DBMS & Backend 
• 
Project Explanation: I spent about 10 minutes clearly explaining my project. Since I 
mentioned handling the backend and database, the interviewer asked for more details. 
I gave him an overview of the backend operations, including how my PHP scripts 
worked and interacted with the database. 
• 
Database Schema: He then asked me to explain my database schema. I detailed the 
structure of each table and explained the reasoning behind my normalization choices. 
This was followed by a question about the foreign key relationships I had 
implemented. 
• 
On-the-Spot Database Design: The interviewer then proposed a new project idea 
and asked me to design a database for it. 
o I started by asking clarifying questions to understand the project's 
requirements and finalize the necessary tables. 
o I designed each table with relevant columns and constraints, periodically 
checking in with the interviewer to ensure my understanding was correct. 
o After structuring the tables, he asked me to normalize the design. I explained 
my normalization strategy and justified my decisions. 
o Finally, he asked me to add foreign key relationships where needed, and I 
identified and included one. 
• 
SQL Queries: To conclude this section, he asked me to write several SQL queries for 
the database we had just designed. The queries primarily involved the use of JOINs 
and subqueries. 
Frontend & Other Technologies 
• 
React/Angular: The interviewer asked if I knew React or Angular. I was honest and 
stated that I was not very familiar with them. 
• 
HTML/CSS Task: He then roughly sketched a webpage design and asked me to 
write the basic HTML and CSS for it, which I did. 
Project 2: Python (DSA) 
• 
The discussion moved to my second project, which was written in Python and 
involved data structures and algorithms. 
• 
Shortest Path Algorithm: The project used a shortest-path algorithm. He asked 
which one I implemented, and I replied that I used Dijkstra's algorithm. 
• 
He followed up by asking why I chose Dijkstra's over other algorithms. I explained 
my reasoning and then named other shortest-path algorithms I knew, such as 
Bellman-Ford, explaining each one briefly. 
General CS Fundamentals 
• 
Java: He asked about my experience with Java. I explained that I was currently 
learning it as part of my semester coursework but was not yet proficient. 
• 
Sorting Algorithms: He then asked if I knew sorting algorithms. I said yes, and he 
specifically asked me to explain Radix Sort. I told him I was more familiar with 
Merge Sort and Quick Sort. He then asked me to explain Quick Sort, which I did with 
a diagram and an example. 
• 
Data Structures: The interviewer presented a scenario and asked which data 
structure I would use to solve it. I suggested a Queue, explained my reasoning, and 
compared it with other data structures to justify why it was the most suitable choice. 
• 
Object-Oriented Programming (OOP): Finally, he asked if I knew OOP concepts. I 
had prepared a concise, 10-minute explanation with a strong example, which I 
delivered clearly. He was impressed and appreciated how well I explained it. 
Conclusion & Feedback 
At the end of the interview, he asked if I had any questions for him. I had prepared two in 
advance: 
1. "How did I perform in the interview?" 
o He responded, "You are strong in what you know, and you know your 
strengths." 
2. "What areas should I improve on?" 
o He answered, "There is nothing you should improve; you are good. However, 
it would be helpful for your career in the industry to get to know more 
technologies like React and Angular." 
Round 3: HR Interview 
This was the final stage. Although it felt like a casual conversation, it was also an elimination 
round. The interview lasted for about 15-20 minutes. 
To my surprise, I was called in 9th out of the 11 candidates, even though I was second on the 
shortlist. When I entered, the HR manager mentioned he did this intentionally to see if 
waiting would make me nervous. 
Interview Discussion & Questions 
The conversation covered a mix of standard HR questions and personal topics: 
• 
Knowledge about Citi: He asked what I knew about the company. I shared 
information I had gathered from a senior who currently works there. He then asked if 
I had attended the Pre-Placement Talk (PPT). I answered honestly that while I had 
joined the session, I was traveling and couldn't pay full attention. 
• 
Motivation: He asked the classic question, "Why do you want to work at Citi?" 
• 
Future Aspirations: He asked, "Where do you see yourself in the future?" 
• 
Personal Interests: My resume mentioned an award in football. As a fellow football 
enthusiast, the HR manager started a friendly discussion about the sport, which helped 
build a good rapport. 
Conclusion & Feedback 
As the interview concluded, the HR manager gave me two key pieces of feedback: 
1. He advised that I should research a company more thoroughly before an interview. 
2. He shared that my technical interviewer had given "great feedback" on my 
performance. I asked him to pass on my thanks. 
I left the interview a little worried that my answer about the company wasn't strong enough. 
However, the results were announced an hour later. 
Final Result 
Out of the 11 candidates who made it to the final round, 7 were selected for the internship, 
including me.  
General Tips 
1. Be strong in DSA, DBMS, OOP, and OS. 
2. Create a clear resume and know every detail on it. 
3. Prepare explanations for your projects and a short pitch for each skill. 
4. Be honest and show them you are a capable candidate. 
 
All the very best!!
