DSA Question-1 :- 
Question 
A robot starts at the top-left corner of an m x n factory floor grid and needs to 
reach the bottom-right corner. The robot can only move in two directions: either one 
step to the right or one step down. The task was to determine the total number of 
unique paths the robot can take to reach the destination. The input consisted of two 
integers representing the number of rows and columns, and the output was the total 
number of unique paths possible under the given movement constraints. 
Approach 
I identified this as a combinatorics problem. To reach the destination, the 
robot must make (m-1) downward moves and (n-1) rightward moves, i.e., (m+n-2) 
total moves in any order. Thus, the problem reduces to computing the binomial 
coefficient C(m+n-2, m-1). I implemented this in C++ using an iterative formula for 
binomial coefficients to avoid overflow from factorials. This gave an efficient solution 
with O(min(m, n)) time complexity and O(1) space. 
Result 
 
In the First Attempt itself, the code had passed all the Test Cases and 
successfully submitted the question and moved on to next. 
 
DSA Question-2 :- 
Question 
Another OA problem was about creating a balanced fertilizer mix. A farmer has N 
organic substances, each with two properties: 
 
Growth Boost (G) → a multiplicative factor that boosts plant growth 
 
Soil Toxicity (T) → an additive factor that harms soil quality 
The goal was to select at least one substance such that the absolute difference 
between the total growth boost (product of selected G values) and the total toxicity 
(sum of selected T values) is minimized. 
The input consisted of an integer N followed by N pairs of integers (G, T), and the 
output was the minimum possible difference across all valid combinations. 
Approach 
Since N ≤ 10, I solved it using a bitmask subset approach. 
 
For each non-empty subset of substances, I computed: 
 Total Growth as the product of selected G values. 
 Total Toxicity as the sum of selected T values. 
 
I then tracked the absolute difference between them and updated the 
minimum difference. 
 
This works efficiently because there are only 2^N - 1 subsets (at most 1023 
when N=10). 
The solution had O(2^N * N) time complexity (iterating through subsets and 
elements) and O(1) extra space. 
Result 
 
The Code had only passed 11 out of 12 Test Cases and due to time constraint, 
I later moved on to next question. 
 
 
DSA Question-3 :- 
Question 
One of the OA problems was to implement a mentor allotment system in a 
consulting organization. Each employee is described by two attributes: skill level (A) 
and project exposure (B). An employee can be mentored only by someone with 
equal or higher skill and exposure. Among all valid mentors, the one with the 
smallest exposure difference is chosen, and if there’s a tie, the smallest skill 
difference is used. Each mentor can guide only one employee, and if a seeker re-
queries, their previous mentor is permanently barred from mentoring anyone else. 
The input consisted of a series of operations: E A B when a new employee joins and 
Q i when the i-th employee requests mentorship. The task was to output the most 
suitable mentor’s ID or "NE" if none exist. 
Approach 
Since the constraints allowed up to 22,000 operations, a brute-force search for each 
query would be inefficient. To solve this, I used a segment tree of sets: 
 
Employees were stored in the tree based on skill levels, with each node 
maintaining a sorted set of {exposure, id} pairs. 
 
When a new employee joined, they were inserted into the tree. When an 
employee queried for a mentor, their data was temporarily removed to avoid 
self-selection. 
 
For queries, I searched in the range of skills ≥ seeker’s skill and used 
lower_bound to quickly find the first mentor with exposure ≥ seeker’s 
exposure. Tie-breaking was done using exposure first, then skill difference. 
 
Once a mentor was assigned, they were removed from the tree to ensure one-
to-one matching. If the seeker re-queried, the old mentor was permanently 
blocked. 
This approach ensured efficient handling of joins and queries in O(log A) per 
operation, with overall complexity around O(N log A) and space O(N).

Interview Rounds :- 
After three days on 30 July 2025, the interview shortlist was released, and 30 
candidates were selected for the next rounds—19 from MIT and 11 from CEG. However, 
since the Appian interview took place before the Goldman Sachs interview, two of the 
shortlisted candidates had already been selected by Appian. As a result, only 28 
candidates attended the Goldman Sachs interview on 11th August 2025. 
There were three interview rounds in total—two technical rounds and one 
resume-based technical + HR round. The process was organized across three panels, with 
each panel handling a different round for a candidate. For example, if a candidate 
attended Round 1 in Panel A, then they would attend Round 2 in Panel B and Round 3 in 
Panel C. In my case, I attended Round 1 in Panel C, Round 2 in Panel B, and Round 3 in 
Panel A. 
Interview Round 1 :- 
 
Round 1 was conducted in two batches. 15 candidates attended the morning 
session, while I was part of the afternoon batch of 13 candidates. In the morning batch, 
out of 15 participants, 4 were shortlisted for Round 2. During that time, the rest of us 
were asked to wait and instructed to go for lunch while those 4 attended their next 
round. 
I attended my Round 1 interview at 3:00 PM, and it lasted for about 30 minutes. 
The interviewer began by asking me to introduce myself, where I highlighted my 
academic background, technical skills, and problem-solving experience. After this, he 
asked if I was familiar with Sudoku. I replied yes, and he then asked me to explain the 
rules of the game. Once I explained, he acknowledged my response and gave me a DSA 
problem based on it — Valid Sudoku, where the task was to check whether a given 
solved Sudoku is valid or not. 
I explained  my approach step by step: I planned to use static 2D arrays to check 
for duplicates in rows, columns, and 3x3 fields. For this, I created three 9x9 arrays, 
where each array tracked whether a number had already been used in a particular row, 
column, or field. I then demonstrated the logic, showing how I would store and check 
values in each structure. For the fields, I calculated the field number and applied the 
same duplicate-checking logic. Although the interviewer expected a set-based approach, 
I clarified that my static array method also worked correctly, and he was satisfied with 
my explanation. 
Next, I discussed the time and space complexity of my solution: O(n²) for 
iterating, storing, and checking, with space complexity of O(3·n²), which reduces to O(1) 
for a fixed-size Sudoku (9x9). As a follow-up, he asked about the property of n, to which 
I answered that n should be a perfect square number. Toward the end, he asked me if I 
had any questions for him, and I specifically asked about the GS Legend platform and its 
role in open-source contributions. He explained it briefly and concluded the round by 
wishing me good luck for the next one. I came out feeling confident that I had 
performed well in this round and had been selected for Round 2. 
Interview Round 2 :-      
In the morning batch of Round 1, 4 candidates were shortlisted for Round 2, 
and in the afternoon batch, 3 more were shortlisted, including myself. My Round 2 
interview started around 4:15 PM and lasted for about 40 minutes. At the 
beginning, the interviewer asked me to introduce myself and then requested me to 
explain the projects I had mentioned in my resume. I walked him through my 
projects, after which he asked a few simple clarifying questions on them. 
The first DSA problem was the Airport–Plane problem: there are multiple 
airports connected by planes, with a cost associated with traveling between them. 
The task was to start from one airport, visit all airports exactly once, and return the 
shortest possible cost, while also explaining the data structure and algorithm to 
implement this. I immediately recognized this as the Travelling Salesman Problem 
(TSP). I explained that the problem can be modeled as a Graph, where airports are 
nodes and planes with costs are weighted edges. I then described the TSP approach, 
explained its working, and wrote pseudocode for the solution. I also mentioned that 
the time complexity of the exact solution using dynamic programming is O(n² · 2ⁿ), 
which becomes infeasible for larger values of n, and that approximate/heuristic 
methods are often used in practice. The interviewer was satisfied with my 
explanation and pseudocode. 
The second problem was about a server handling requests with a fixed buffer size. If 
extra requests arrive when the buffer is full, the server should reply with a message 
that it is full. Each request is handled in a fixed time slot, and after each time slot, the 
next request is served. I identified this as a Queue-based problem. I explained that I 
would enqueue requests as they arrive, respond with a “server full” message if the 
buffer is at capacity, and dequeue a request after every time slot. The time 
complexity for both enqueue and dequeue operations is O(1). Additionally, I 
optimized the dequeuing logic by maintaining a time variable and a time array that 
tracks which requests must be dequeued at each time slot. With this, the time 
pointer simply moves to the next slot for automatic dequeuing in O(1). I further 
explained that we could even optimize by storing the next request’s dequeue time in 
the array itself. The interviewer appreciated this detailed explanation and said my 
logic was correct. 
Toward the end, he asked if I had any questions. I asked about the purpose behind 
setting up the Goldman Sachs Hyderabad office, which was recently opened in 
2021. He explained the reason and wished me all the best for the next round. I came 
out of the room feeling happy and confident that I would be shortlisted for the 
following round.

Personal Tips :- 
 Know your resume well – this is the personal mantra. 
 Practice DSA using LeetCode 150 and Striver’s DSA Sheet. 
 Engage with a senior from 2nd year onwards for intern preparation – in 
my case, a senior from my college guided me throughout. 
 Communicate clearly – explain every step of your thinking while solving 
problems. 
 Use your 2nd-year summer holidays wisely for dedicated internship 
preparation. 
 Don’t get demotivated if others move ahead before you. For example, 
some attended Round 2 in the morning while we were still waiting for 
Round 1 in the afternoon. Ultimately, it’s not first-come-first-serve; they 
select the best performers, and most of the afternoon candidates were 
chosen in the end. 
 Stay connected with your seniors and clear doubts regularly. 
 For OOPS, DBMS, OS, and CN, attend college lectures sincerely – they 
cover enough to build a strong base. For further depth, refer to online 
resources and notes
