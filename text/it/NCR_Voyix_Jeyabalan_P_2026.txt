Round 1 : Online coding round:

[1 hr ]

The online coding test was conducted at CUIC through the HackerRank platform. We were
given two coding problems—one easy and one hard. The harder problem was challenging for
most candidates, so it’s completely normal even if you couldn’t solve it fully.

Question 1:
 Link: https://leetcode.com/discuss/post/7366207/atlassian-question-how-to-solve-this-by-rjuij/
 Tip: I initially attempted a graph-traversal + factorial-based approach, which gives the correct
logic but exceeds the time limit due to constraints. The optimal solution involves using
combinatorics, which is more efficient and runs in roughly O(n log n) time.

Question 2:
 You are given a set of points on a Cartesian plane. These points collectively form multiple
squares, and in a few squares one point is missing. The task is to find and return the missing
point for incomplete squares.
 Tip: This can be solved cleanly using an XOR-based method or by using set operations to
identify the missing coordinate.

Round 2 : Technical Interview(Offline):

[ 1 hr ]

I had a panel of two interviewers. The discussion began with my self-introduction, after

which I walked them through a few of my projects. They were genuinely interested, and I
explained one of the projects in depth. Most of the questions at this stage were focused on the
work I had done, the technical decisions I had taken, and the outcomes.

Next, the interview moved into DSA. They referred to the online assessment questions,

and I was asked to pick one and explain it. I chose the hard problem, described my initial
approach, clearly justified why it failed due to time complexity, and then walked them through
the optimal approach. They also asked for the time complexity, and I explained that along with
writing the pseudo-code on paper.

There was a whiteboard with partially erased Python code written on it. I was asked to

interpret the code and explain what it might be doing. The twist was that even they did not know
the original code (it was pre-written); the intention was simply to test how I approach an
unfamiliar or incomplete task.

Next came a system/design question: “Consider you are designing a WhatsApp-like

application. Design the database.”
 I identified the stakeholders, listed the entities, and drew an ER diagram. They further asked
how the user messages table would look, so I designed the schema, taking into account both
user-to-user and user-to-group messaging. I ensured my design satisfied 3NF.

We also briefly discussed end-to-end encryption and how messages remain secure. This

led to a conversation on JWT, which I explained in detail.

Preparation Strategy:

For DSA, I mainly followed Striver’s SDE Sheet and the NeetCode 150 list. I also made it

a habit to solve daily LeetCode problems and regularly participate in LeetCode contests. (I
personally wouldn’t recommend Codeforces for interview preparation as it is more
competitive-programming–oriented and not fully aligned with typical interview patterns.)

For DBMS, I practiced the LeetCode SQL 50 problems and HackerRank SQL
challenges. I focused strongly on topics like joins, subqueries, CASE/IF, window functions,
normalization, and database design, as these commonly appear in interviews.

For the core CS subjects, I didn’t follow separate dedicated material. Instead, I revised
important concepts from Operating Systems and Computer Networks using quick summaries
and explanations from ChatGPT.
