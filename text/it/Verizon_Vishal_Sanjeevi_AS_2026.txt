Recruitment Process Experience:

The placement drive was a multi-stage process that tested various skills, from technical knowledge to
soft skills.

1. Online Test

The online test consisted of four sections.

  Essay Typing: We were given a topic and had to write an essay of 190-240 words in 25 minutes.
This tested our writing and time management skills.Topic given to me was ”Are Electric cars
the future”.

  GenAI  MCQs:  This  section  had  30  multiple-choice  questions  focused  on  Generative  AI,

including scenario-based questions. The time limit was 30 minutes.

  Coding Round(40 Minutes): There were two coding questions. The first was to determine if a
number is "googly," which is true if the sum of its digits is a prime number. The second was a
Dijkstra-based problem about  finding the  minimum  time to collect  energy  while  traversing
cities. My understanding of graph algorithms was crucial here.

  English/Vocabulary Assessment: This section had a flexible number of questions and featured
negative marking. A unique aspect was the '?' option, which you could select if you weren't
sure of an answer to avoid negative marks. This section also included simple and enjoyable
puzzles. The more correct answers you gave, the higher your level and score.

The results for the online round were announced two days after the test. Out of 322 candidates, 80
were shortlisted for the interview round.

2. Group Discussion (GD) and AI Prompting Round

The next day, we had the GD and AI prompting rounds.

  Group  Discussion:  We  were  divided  into  teams  of  9,  and  the  discussion  lasted  about  25
minutes. The topic was "Are online ways of life (online education, hybrid work, e-commerce,
etc.) better or not?" The key here was the quality of your speech and your active participation.
The  panel  judged  us  on  how  well  we  involved  ourselves  in  the  discussion  and  contributed
meaningfully, rather than just stating a couple of points and staying silent. From my panel, 7
out of 9 were selected, while other panels had a higher rejection rate.

  AI  Prompting  Round:  This  was  a  non-eliminatory  round.  We  had  to  choose  a  problem
statement  from  a  given  list,  write  a  prompt  to  solve  it  using  an  AI  tool,  and  explain  the
prompting  technique  used.  We  also  had  to  mention  any  previous  AI/ML  projects  we  had
worked on. It's important to be fully knowledgeable about what you submit in this round, as
it formed the basis for many questions in the technical interview. Having AI/ML projects on
your resume definitely adds more value.

3. Technical Interview

The technical interview lasted for about an hour. After a brief self-introduction and introduction from
the interviewer, he delved into my projects, showing a particular interest in my final year project. We
had a deep, 20-minute discussion on it, and he seemed impressed.

Next,  the  interviewer  transitioned  to  my  responses  from  the  AI  Prompting  round.  I  had  chosen  a
problem statement related to cyber security, and he asked me basic questions on the topic to gauge
my foundational knowledge. He also asked me to explain in detail why I had framed the prompt the
way  I  did  and  to  walk  him  through  my  entire  approach.  This  part  of  the  interview  reinforced  the
importance of being completely aware of what you submit in the AI prompting round.

He then moved on to DSA, asking a basic string-based palindrome question. He also asked for a dry
run of Binary Search, its time complexity, and how that complexity is derived. The same was asked for
Merge Sort. The interview then shifted to SQL. I was asked to explain all the different types of JOINS
with proper examples and to differentiate between a LEFT JOIN and an OUTER JOIN. He then gave me
a medium-level SQL question involving two tables. I couldn't provide a complete solution, but I politely
explained that I was unable to come up with one, and he was fine with it. The interview concluded
with questions about my knowledge of Verizon and my motivation for wanting to join the company. I

asked  two  sensible,  high-quality  questions,  which  is  always  a  good  practice.  Of  the  80  shortlisted
students, 32 were selected for the HR round.

4. HR Interview

The HR interview was straightforward and lasted about 15 minutes. He asked me to briefly explain my
projects,  inquired  about my  family background,  asked  the  typical “Where  do you see yourself in 5
years?” question, my location preference and again, asked why I wanted to join Verizon. A crucial tip
here is to be flexible about location. When asked for a location preference, it's best to say you are
open to any location based on the company's requirements, as rejections can happen due to this. All
32 students who made it to the HR round were selected, and I was one of them.

MY PREPARATION STRATEGY:

I began my preparation during my 6th-semester holidays, focusing on a strategic approach to cover
essential topics. My main focus was on Data Structures and Algorithms (DSA), particularly Arrays and
Strings, as well as Greedy algorithms and Dynamic Programming (DP). I also made sure to learn the
fundamentals of Trees and Graphs.

I highly recommend utilizing Striver's A to Z DSA sheet and his video tutorials from TakeYouForward.
This  resource  proved  to  be  extremely  helpful.  For  practice,  I  focused  on  the  LeetCode  150  and
LeetCode 75 lists, which helped me build a strong problem-solving foundation. In addition to DSA, I
extensively practiced SQL queries, solving easy and medium-level questions, which gave me a lot of
confidence in this area.I learnt all the core concepts from GeekForGeeks and InterviewBit.

One of the most important aspects of preparation is maintaining a  competitive spirit.  I found that
learning  and  practicing  with  friends  was  far more  effective  than  studying  alone.  This  collaborative
environment kept me motivated and helped me improve faster. It's also worth  noting that while a
good  CGPA  is  advisable  for  eligibility,  typically  an  8.0  and  above  is  a  safe  bet,  it's  the  skills  you
demonstrate that matter most in the end.

TIPS and TRICKS:

Focus on Fundamentals: A strong foundation in Data Structures and Algorithms (DSA),
particularly Arrays and Strings, is essential as most of the companies focus on these. Practice
problems on platforms like LeetCode,Hackerrank etc.

Be a Team Player: Learning and preparing with friends fosters a competitive and supportive
environment that makes the process more enjoyable and effective.

Be Honest: If you don't know the solution to a problem, it's better to be honest and polite rather
than trying to fake it.

Ask Quality Questions: Always prepare and ask good, sensible questions to the interviewer at
the end. It shows your genuine interest in the role and the company.

Master Your Communication Skills: Strong communication is crucial. Make sure you can articulate
your  thoughts  clearly  and  confidently.  Practice  explaining  your  projects  and  problem-solving
approaches to others.

Showcase  Flagship  Projects: Instead of  listing  every project  you've  ever worked  on, focus on your
flagship projects. These are the projects where you made a significant contribution to the real world
and that provide genuine value. Be prepared to discuss these in detail, as they will be a focal point of
your interviews.

Hope this detailed breakdown of my experience helps you in your preparation.

All the best!
